Why This Exists

I was tracking 40 applications in a spreadsheet. It was lying to me.

Every cell said "Applied" or "Interviewing." But half of them had been silently rejected — the emails were buried in a Gmail inbox I was too exhausted to check properly. The other half had recruiter emails I'd missed because they landed in Promotions.

I didn't need a prettier spreadsheet. I needed something that watches my inbox and tells me what's actually happening — without me maintaining it.

That's foray.

The Problem

Your spreadsheet doesn't read your email.

How It Works

Four-stage email pipeline. Rules-first, LLM as fallback.

ingest
Fetch from Gmail
every 15 min
→
match
Link email to
application
→
classify
Regex rules first,
LLM fallback
→
act
Auto-update status
or review queue
The key insight: most rejection and interview emails follow predictable templates. Regex catches 80%+ of them with zero API cost. Only ambiguous emails go to Claude Haiku — keeping LLM spend under $0.50/day. High-confidence classifications auto-update status. Low-confidence ones land in a review queue, typically 0–3 items per day.
Under the Hood

What foray does when an email arrives

classifier pipeline
$ ingest — fetch new email from Gmail
gmail_message_id: 18e4a2b3c7f9d012
subject: "Thank you for your interest in Stripe"
from: noreply@stripe.com
 
$ match — find linked application
matched: Stripe / Senior PM (application #12)
 
$ classify — rules-first, LLM fallback
rule_id: rejection_template_v3
label: rejection
confidence: 0.94
classified_by: rules
 
$ act — apply status change
canonical_status: interviewing → rejected
event: auto_status_changed (undoable=true)
✓ done. undo available for 10s.
Features

What you get

Capture in seconds

Manual form with company autocomplete, role title, URL, JD paste, salary range. One transaction creates the application and its first event.

Your inbox, triaged

Gmail polling every 15 minutes. Each email classified as rejection, interview invite, recruiter outreach, noise, or unmatched.

Status updates itself

High-confidence classifications auto-update application status. Every change is undoable. Status regressions always need human confirmation.

Review queue

Low-confidence emails surface in a focused queue. Typically 0–3 items per day. First 50 emails always go to review for ground truth.

Campaign room view

Application list filterable by canonical status. Detail view with chronological timeline — stages, events, linked emails. Not a spreadsheet. A room you walk into every morning to see what's moving.

Architecture
Vertical Slice
(VSA)

Key decisions: Vertical Slice Architecture — feature-first, not layer-first. tenantDb(userId) wrapper for multi-tenant safety; every query auto-injects userId. Branded types for IDs so the compiler catches tenant mixups. Result<T, AppError> via neverthrow instead of throwing. Rules-first classifier keeps LLM costs near zero. Local-first by design — Docker Postgres, no cloud dependency.
